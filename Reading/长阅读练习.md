## Passage — The Rise of Urban Rooftop Farming

**A**  
In cities from Singapore to New York, a quiet revolution is taking place above street level. Rooftop farms—ranging from simple herb gardens to fully operational vegetable and fruit plots—have grown in number over the past fifteen years. Supporters argue that they reduce the distance food travels, provide fresh produce for local restaurants and residents, and help to lower the urban heat island effect by replacing dark, heat-absorbing surfaces with vegetation. Critics, however, point out that the yields are often small, the set-up and maintenance costs can be high, and that not every building structure can safely support the weight of soil, water, and crops.

**B**  
The idea is not new. During the two world wars, "victory gardens" and similar projects encouraged city dwellers to grow food on any available patch of land, including rooftops, to ease pressure on the food supply. After the wars, many of these gardens disappeared as industrial agriculture and long-distance distribution became the norm. The current revival is linked to concerns about climate change, food security, and a desire for more transparent supply chains. Some city governments now offer grants or tax incentives to building owners who install green roofs or rooftop farms, as part of broader sustainability targets.

**C**  
Not every rooftop is suitable. Engineers must assess the load-bearing capacity of the structure, access to water and electricity, and exposure to wind and sunlight. In colder climates, the growing season may be short without additional heating or enclosed spaces, which again raises costs. Nevertheless, a number of commercial rooftop farms have proven viable. For example, Brooklyn Grange in New York operates two rooftop sites totaling more than two acres, producing tens of thousands of kilograms of organic produce each year and supplying restaurants, farmers’ markets, and community-supported agriculture (CSA) schemes. Similar projects exist in cities such as London, Paris, and Tokyo.

**D**  
Rooftop farming also has an educational and social dimension. Schools and community groups often use rooftop gardens to teach children and adults about where food comes from and how it is grown. In some neighbourhoods, rooftop plots are used to train unemployed or disadvantaged people in horticulture and basic business skills, with the aim of improving their job prospects. Such programmes may receive funding from local councils or charities. The produce is sometimes sold at low cost to nearby residents or donated to food banks, thus addressing both employment and food access issues.

**E**  
Whether rooftop farming will ever supply a large share of a city’s food is doubtful. Most studies suggest that urban agriculture—including rooftop and other inner-city growing—can only ever meet a small fraction of urban food demand. Its main benefits are therefore not necessarily volume, but rather education, community building, greener cities, and a symbolic step towards reconnecting people with the source of their food. For many city planners and residents, that is already enough to justify the space and investment.

## 题目 (Questions 1–8)

### Questions 1–5 — True / False / Not Given

根据文章判断下列句子。选 **TRUE**、**FALSE** 或 **NOT GIVEN**。

1. Rooftop farms can help reduce the urban heat island effect.  T
2. Rooftop farming was more common during the world wars than it is today.  T
3. Some governments give financial support to building owners who set up rooftop farms.  T
4. Brooklyn Grange grows only vegetables, not fruit.  NG
5. Rooftop farming is expected to provide most of a city’s food in the future.F

---

### Questions 6–8 — 填空 (Summary completion)

从文章中选词填空，使下列摘要与原文一致。**每空不超过两个词**。

Rooftop farming has **6** ________ and social benefits: for example, schools use rooftop gardens to teach people about food and **7** ________. Some projects train disadvantaged people in horticulture to improve their **8** ________. The produce may be sold cheaply or given to food banks.

## 答案与解析 (Answers and Brief Notes)

### Questions 1–5 (T/F/NG)

| 题号 | 答案 | 解析 |
|------|------|------|
| 1 | **TRUE** | 段落 A："... help to lower the urban heat island effect by replacing ... with vegetation." 与题干一致。 |
| 2 | **NOT GIVEN** | 段落 B 说的是：战争时很常见 → **战后**（after the wars）很多消失了（减少了）。题目问的是「战争时 vs **今天**」：今天有 **the current revival**（复兴），文章没说过今天的规模有没有超过或达到战争时，所以只能选 NG。**减少是「战后」相对「战争时」，不是「今天」相对「战争时」。** |
| 3 | **TRUE** | 段落 B："Some city governments now offer grants or tax incentives to building owners who install green roofs or rooftop farms." 即政府给业主资金或税务优惠。 |
| 4 | **NOT GIVEN** | 段落 C 只说 Brooklyn Grange 生产 "organic produce" 和 "vegetable and fruit" 在段落 A 是泛指 rooftop farms，没有说 Brooklyn Grange 只种蔬菜不种水果。 |
| 5 | **FALSE** | 段落 E："Whether rooftop farming will ever supply a large share of a city's food is doubtful." 以及 "can only ever meet a small fraction of urban food demand"，与「提供大部分城市食物」矛盾。 |

### Questions 6–8 (Summary)

| 题号 | 答案 | 解析 |
|------|------|------|
| 6 | **educational** | 段落 D 首句："Rooftop farming also has an **educational** and social dimension." 摘要里先填 educational，与 social 对应。 |
| 7 | **how grown** 或 **its growth** | 段落 D："... teach ... about where food comes from and **how it is grown**." 题目要求每空不超过两词，故答 **how grown** 或 **its growth**。 |
| 8 | **job prospects** | 段落 D："... with the aim of improving their **job prospects**." |

## 小结

- **T/F/NG**：看到「比较」「只/全部」「大部分」要回原文找有没有**明确比较/范围**，没有就选 **NOT GIVEN**。  
- **Summary**：填空多为原文原词或轻微变形（如 improve → improving），先定位段落再选词。  
- 长阅读练习可以每段读完后用一句话概括段落大意，方便做题时定位。
